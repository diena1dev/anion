# Reactor Types (non-steam)

Source: googol ai

Common sci-fi reactor types that do not generate steam:

## 1. Direct Conversion / MHD (Magnetohydrodynamic) Generators

**How it works:** Instead of capturing heat, these reactors heat a gas until it becomes a highly ionized plasma.
This charged plasma is shot at high velocities through a powerful magnetic field. By Faraday's Law of Induction,
the moving conductive plasma induces a direct electrical current in the surrounding magnetic coils, drawn off as power.

**Real-world connection:** Researchers and aerospace engineers frequently utilize theoretical MHD
and Direct Energy Conversion (DEC) to bypass the inefficiencies of steam turbines.

## 2. Cyclotronic / Pulsed Fusion

**How it works:** Modeled on concepts like the Field-Reversed Configuration (FRC),
these reactors fire rapid pulses of plasma rather than maintaining a steady, hot burn.
When the fusion reaction occurs, the expanding hot plasma pushes outward against an immense magnetic field.
Just as regenerative braking in electric cars captures kinetic energy, the expanding magnetic flux induces a surge of current in the surrounding circuits.

## 3. Thermionic and Betavoltaic Generators

**How it works:** Thermionic generators function on the principle of the Edison effect.
An emitter plate is heated to extreme temperatures by a reactor core (advanced fission or fusion source),
causing electrons to boil off and cross a vacuum gap to a cooler collector plate — directly generating voltage.
Betavoltaics use semiconductor junctions to directly absorb beta particles (electrons) emitted by decaying radioactive isotopes, with no moving parts.

## 4. Hypermatter & Zero-Point Energy (ZPE)

**How it works:** Staple technologies of the far future (e.g., Star Wars hypermatter reactors).
They tap into the vacuum energy of quantum foam or alternate dimensions rather than burning traditional nuclear fuel.
Because they pull raw electrical or spacetime-bending potential directly from the universe, they need no thermodynamic heat engines.
